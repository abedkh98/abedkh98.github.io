Engineering philosophy

How I approach hard problems.

Four principles that show up in every project — from PCB layout to clinical protocol design.

⬡

Start with the patient

Every system begins with a clinical problem, not a technical one. The technology should be invisible — what matters is whether a stroke survivor can lift their arm after 8 weeks. I've watched participants cry when they do things they haven't done in years. That's the metric that counts.

◈

Close the loop

Open-loop systems are guesses. I build feedback-driven systems that sense, decode, decide, and act in real time — from adaptive spinal stimulators that respond to EMG intent, to sleep-stage therapies triggered by brain oscillations. The biology provides the signal; the engineering provides the response.

⬡

Build it, then break it

I've designed PCBs, written firmware, built ML pipelines, and run clinical trials — sometimes on the same project. If it doesn't survive a patient's home for 8 weeks, it doesn't exist yet. 21 hardware iterations on our sensor platform weren't failures — they were data.

◈

Ship a working prototype

Papers matter. Patents matter. But a blinking LED on a breadboard you built yourself tells you something a simulation never will. The constraint of real hardware forces decisions. I'd rather have a flawed working system than a perfect theoretical one.

The engineering journey

2011–2013

First closed loop

Adaptive neuro-fuzzy controllers for FES, restoring walking in rodent paralysis models. First encounter with the frustrating magic of biological systems.

2013–2018

Decoding the motor cortex

PhD: real-time LFP decoding for robotic arm control. Learned that noise is biology's way of telling you your model is wrong.

2017–2018

Brain to spinal cord

University of Washington: co-built an FPGA-based brain-to-spine interface. Saw a rat regain 38% of forelimb movement. That result shaped everything that followed.

2021–2025

From animal models to patients

Northwestern: ran a 59-patient RCT with MINT. Went from idea → wearable system → home deployment → published trial. 7.8× better recovery.

2025–now

Closing the loop at the spinal cord

Non-invasive closed-loop spinal stimulation driven by decoded EMG intent — and AI coaching systems that deliver therapy at home, in real time.
